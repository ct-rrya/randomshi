Dejito, Bea
Topic: Business Processes — Definition & Types
Business Processes

What Are Business Processes?

Before we understand what IT does inside an organization, we need to understand what the organization does first. Every company, school, government, or institution runs on business processes — the structured series of steps that people or systems carry out to produce a specific result.[1]

Business Process: A collection of related, structured activities or tasks performed by people or systems to deliver a specific service, product, or outcome for a defined audience.[2]

Why This Matters to IT

IT does not exist in isolation. Every IT decision — what software to deploy, what servers to maintain, what to secure — must map to what the organization is actually trying to accomplish. An IT professional who does not understand business processes will build things nobody needs, or miss the real problem entirely.[1]

Three Types of Business Processes

Core Processes

Directly create value for the customer or client. Examples: delivering a product, processing an order, teaching a class, treating a patient.

Support Processes

Enable core processes to run smoothly but do not directly produce the end product. Examples: HR, IT infrastructure, payroll, facilities management.

Management Processes

Govern and oversee the whole organization. Examples: strategic planning, compliance monitoring, performance reviews, budgeting.

Key Characteristics of a Business Process

  • Defined Input & Output — Every process starts with something (a request, data, raw material) and ends with something (a report, a product, a service).
  • Repeatable — The same process can be performed consistently across many instances.
  • Measurable — You can track how long it takes, how much it costs, and how often it succeeds.
  • Value-Adding — The output should be worth more to the recipient than the inputs were to begin with.
Discussion Questions
  1. Name one core, one support, and one management process at CTU-Daanbantayan.
  2. If a business process cannot be measured, can IT truly support it? Why or why not?

Topic: Business Processes — How IT Supports Them & Real Examples
IT & Business

How IT Supports Business Processes

Knowing what a business process is only gets you halfway. The more important question for an IT professional is: how does technology make that process faster, more accurate, more accessible, or more secure?[1]

Core Idea: IT is a means to an end. The "end" is always the business process. IT professionals serve business goals — not the other way around.[3]

Five Ways IT Supports Processes

  • Automation — Eliminates repetitive manual tasks. Example: automated payroll calculation, inventory tracking.[1]
  • Communication — Email, messaging tools, video conferencing. Teams coordinate without being physically together.[3]
  • Data Management — Databases store and retrieve critical information instantly, accurately, and at scale.[4]
  • Decision Support — Analytics dashboards and reports give managers real information to act on — not gut feeling.[1]
  • Security & Compliance — IT protects process integrity and ensures legal and regulatory requirements are met.[5]

Side-by-Side: With and Without IT

Business Process Without IT With IT Support
Student Enrollment Manual paper forms, long queues, frequent errors in records Online portal, automated slot management, instant confirmation emails
Bank Transaction Teller-only, limited to banking hours, paper receipts 24/7 ATM and mobile banking, real-time balance updates
Product Inventory Manual counting, stock errors, delayed reorders Barcode/RFID scanning, automatic reorder triggers, live stock view
Hospital Patient Records Paper files, risk of loss, slow retrieval across departments Electronic Health Records (EHR), instant cross-department access, audit trails

When IT Fails the Process

IT support can also go wrong. Some common failure modes:

  • Automation without understanding — Automating a broken process just makes you fail faster.
  • Technology-first thinking — Choosing a trendy tool before understanding what the process actually needs.
  • Poor user adoption — A system nobody uses solves nothing, no matter how well-built it is.

  1. Pick one school process you find inefficient. How would you redesign it with IT support?
  2. "Automating a broken process just makes you fail faster." Do you agree? Give an example.

Topic: IT Environment — The 5 Core Components
IT Environment

The IT Environment & Its 5 Components

The IT environment is the complete ecosystem of hardware, software, networks, data, and people that an organization uses to manage information and keep operations running. Think of it as the infrastructure behind everything digital in a workplace.[4]

IT Environment: The full set of infrastructure and systems — physical and logical — that an organization uses to collect, store, process, and communicate information.[3]

The 5 Core Components

1. Hardware

Physical devices: servers, computers, routers, switches, printers, storage drives, IoT devices. The physical foundation everything else depends on.

2. Software

Programs that run on hardware: operating systems, business applications (ERP, CRM), custom-built systems, and utilities. What the hardware actually does.

3. Network

The communication layer: LAN, WAN, Wi-Fi, VPN, internet. Connects all devices and enables data to move between them.

4. Data

The actual information assets: customer records, financial data, documents, logs. Often the most valuable component — and the most targeted by attackers.

5. People

IT professionals, end-users, administrators. Technology is only as effective as the people managing and using it. The most unpredictable component.

Why All 5 Are Interdependent

A failure in any one component cascades through the others. You cannot treat them as separate:

  • Network goes down → Software hosted on servers becomes unreachable → Data is inaccessible → People cannot work.
  • People are untrained → Software is misused → Data becomes corrupted → Hardware resources are wasted.
  • Data is unprotected → Breach occurs → Trust in the entire IT environment collapses.

Doble, Vence Peter
Topic: IT Environment — Infrastructure Types & Security
Infrastructure

Infrastructure Types & Securing the IT Environment

Once you understand the 5 components of an IT environment, the next question is: where does it all live, and who is responsible for keeping it safe?[4] The answer depends on what kind of infrastructure an organization chooses to run.

Types of IT Infrastructure

TypeDescriptionAdvantageRisk
On-Premise All hardware and software are physically located and managed by the organization itself. Full control, no internet dependency High upfront cost, requires in-house IT staff, vulnerable to physical damage
Cloud-Based Resources are hosted by a third-party provider (AWS, Azure, Google) and accessed via the internet. Scalable, lower maintenance, accessible anywhere Internet dependency, data is off-site, shared infrastructure risks
Hybrid A combination: sensitive data stays on-premise, less critical workloads move to the cloud. Flexibility, balance of control and scalability More complex to manage, requires clear data governance policy

Securing Each Layer of the IT Environment

Every component of the IT environment is a potential entry point for attackers. IT professionals must think about security at every layer — not just one:[5]

  • Physical Security — Who can physically access server rooms, devices, and cables? Locks, keycards, CCTV.
  • Network Security — Firewalls, intrusion detection/prevention systems (IDS/IPS), VPNs, network segmentation.
  • Application Security — Patching vulnerabilities, input validation, strong authentication, code reviews.
  • Data Security — Encryption at rest and in transit, access controls, backup and recovery strategies.
  • User/People Security — Security awareness training, phishing simulations, enforcing password policies.
Defense in Depth: No single layer of security is enough. Professional IT security means layering multiple defenses so that if one fails, the next one holds. Attackers have to breach every layer; defenders only have to hold one.

Real Scenario: School Switches to Cloud

Suppose CTU decides to move all student records from a local server to Google Drive / cloud storage. New security questions immediately arise:

  • Who controls access permissions to the cloud storage?
  • What happens to records if the institution's internet connection goes down?
  • Does the cloud provider's data center comply with Philippine data privacy law (RA 10173)?
  • Who monitors for unauthorized access or data leaks in the cloud?
Discussion Questions
  1. If your school had to pick one infrastructure type right now — on-premise, cloud, or hybrid — which would you recommend and why?
  2. Which security layer do you think is most often ignored in Philippine schools? What's the consequence of ignoring it?

Topic: Organizational Culture — Elements & How Culture Affects IT
Org. Culture

Organizational Culture & Its Effect on IT

You can deploy the best technology in the world — and it will still fail if the organization's culture does not support it. Culture is one of the most overlooked but most decisive factors in whether IT succeeds or quietly dies inside an organization.[6]

Organizational Culture: The shared values, beliefs, behaviors, norms, and assumptions that shape how people in an organization think, act, and interact — with each other, and with technology.[7]

Core Elements of Organizational Culture

Values

What the organization believes matters most — innovation, efficiency, tradition, collaboration, hierarchy, or openness.

Norms

Unwritten rules about how things are done. "We never skip approvals, even in emergencies." These override official policies all the time.

Leadership Style

Top-down authority vs. collaborative decision-making directly controls how IT projects get approved, funded, and trusted.

Communication Patterns

Whether teams share information freely or in silos determines how well IT systems get adopted and improved over time.

How Culture Shapes IT — Both Directions

Cultural TraitPositive IT ImpactNegative IT Impact
Innovation-friendly Embraces new tools, fast adoption Reckless experimentation, insecure shortcuts
Risk-averse / traditional Strong compliance, thorough testing Resistance to change, systems become outdated
Collaborative IT gets real-world feedback, issues get reported Decision-making slows down if consensus is always required
Siloed / hierarchical Clear approval chains for IT procurement Poor data sharing across departments, redundant systems

Note that the same cultural trait can be both helpful and harmful to IT — it depends on context, leadership, and how the culture is managed. This is why IT professionals need to read the culture they work in, not just the technical specs.[6]

Discussion Questions
  1. Describe the culture of CTU-Daanbantayan based on what you observe. Does it help or hurt how technology is used here?
  2. Can two organizations use the exact same IT system and get completely different results because of culture? Give a real or hypothetical example.

Topic: Organizational Culture — Change Management & Alignment Strategies
Change Mgmt

Managing Culture When Introducing IT Change

Introducing a new IT system is never purely a technical challenge. It is always, at least equally, a human challenge. Most IT implementations that fail do not fail because the technology is bad — they fail because the people didn't adopt it.[8]

Why People Resist New IT Systems

  • "We've always done it this way" — Long-tenured staff comfortable with existing (often manual) workflows see new systems as threats to their routine.[8]
  • Fear of job displacement — Employees who worry that automation will make their role unnecessary will unconsciously or openly resist IT adoption.[1]
  • Low digital literacy — Staff who lack confidence with technology will find ways to avoid using new systems rather than admit they don't understand them.[6]
  • No leadership buy-in — If management does not visibly use and endorse a new system, employees read this as a signal that it doesn't really matter.[8]
  • Poor communication — If people don't know why a system is changing, they fill the gap with their own assumptions — usually negative ones.[8]

Strategies for Aligning IT with Organizational Culture

  • Involve users early — Include end-users in the design or selection process. People support what they helped create.
  • Communicate the why — Before the how or the what, explain clearly why the change is happening and what problem it solves for the user — not just the organization.
  • Train thoroughly — Rollouts without training are setups for failure. Build in adequate training time and make it easy to ask for help.
  • Start with a pilot group — Roll out to a small, willing group first. Let them become advocates and troubleshooters before the organization-wide launch.
  • Secure visible leadership support — Get management to use the system publicly. Their visible adoption signals to everyone else that this is real and permanent.
  • Celebrate wins — When a team or department successfully adopts the new system, acknowledge it. Positive reinforcement shapes culture.
Key Insight: Part of an IT professional's job is understanding the humans, not just the technology. Successful implementation requires empathy, communication, and stakeholder management — not just technical skill.

Quick Scenario

A company's IT team wants to replace a manual filing cabinet system with a cloud-based Document Management System (DMS). Senior staff are resistant. What cultural factors might be at play, and what would you do?

Cultural FactorLikely ConcernIT Response
Long tenure"I know where everything is. This will slow me down."Involve them in organizing the folder structure in the new system.
Low digital literacy"I don't know how to use cloud software."Provide hands-on 1:1 training before full launch.
Fear of surveillance"They'll see everything I access."Communicate clearly who has access to what and why.

Topic: Professionalism — Traits, Ethics & Professional Behavior
Professionalism

Professionalism in IT

Technical skill gets you into the room. Professionalism determines whether you stay, lead, and get trusted with things that matter. In IT, where professionals routinely handle sensitive data, critical systems, and the digital lives of real people — how you carry yourself is not a soft concern. It is a core competency.[9]

Professionalism: The conduct, behavior, and attitude of a person in accordance with the norms, ethics, and standards of their profession — consistently, not just when observed.[9]

6 Core Traits of a Professional IT Person

Reliability

You do what you said you would do, when you said you would do it. Deadlines and commitments are honored consistently.

Accountability

When something goes wrong, you own it. You don't deflect blame or manufacture excuses. You focus on the fix.

Integrity

You act ethically even when no one is watching — no unauthorized access, no data misuse, no dishonest reporting.

Competence

You keep your skills current. Technology changes constantly — professionals commit to continuous learning, not just past credentials.

Respect

You treat colleagues, clients, and users with dignity regardless of their technical skill level or position.

Confidentiality

IT professionals regularly access sensitive data. Professional behavior means never disclosing what you are not authorized to share.

Professional vs. Unprofessional: In Practice

SituationUnprofessional ResponseProfessional Response
You caused a bug that's affecting users "That's not my fault. I just followed the specs." "I'm sorry — let me investigate and fix this right away."
You access a user's files during maintenance Browse files out of curiosity since you have the access Access only what is necessary; document your actions; report anything unusual
A non-technical client doesn't understand your explanation Get impatient, use more jargon, dismiss their question Rephrase in plain language, use an analogy, ask what part is still unclear
A deadline is at risk due to unexpected issues Stay silent and hope the problem resolves itself Proactively notify stakeholders and present a revised timeline with a clear plan

IT Codes of Ethics

Several global professional bodies have formalized what ethical IT behavior looks like:

  • ACM Code of Ethics — Contributing to society, avoiding harm, honesty, fairness, and respecting privacy.[10]
  • IEEE Code of Ethics — Prioritizing public safety, honesty in claims, avoiding conflicts of interest, supporting professional development.[11]
  • RA 10173 (Data Privacy Act) — Philippine law governing how personal data must be handled, stored, and protected by organizations and IT professionals.[12]

Why Professionalism Is an Organizational Concern

How IT professionals behave shapes how the entire IT department is perceived — and trusted — inside the organization:

  • IT teams that communicate proactively earn budget and leadership trust far more easily.
  • Professionals who speak clearly get included in strategic decisions — not just technical tasks.
  • One serious breach of confidentiality can permanently destroy the trust an IT team spent years building.
  • Unprofessional behavior gets IT sidelined. Professional behavior gets IT a seat at the table.
Discussion Questions
  1. Honestly rate yourself (1–5) on each of the 6 professional traits. Which one needs the most work before you enter the industry?
  2. You discover a security vulnerability in a client's system that your team accidentally introduced. The professional course of action is clear — but it means admitting fault publicly. Walk us through what you would actually do.
  3. Why do "soft skills" like communication, empathy, and ethics matter in a technical field? Can you be a great IT professional without them?
