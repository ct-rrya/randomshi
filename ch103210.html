Topic 1

Business Processes

Before we can understand the role of IT in an organization, we need to understand what an organization does. Every company, school, government agency, or non-profit runs on business processes — the structured series of activities people or systems perform to achieve a specific goal.

Business Process: A collection of related, structured activities or tasks that produce a specific service or product for a particular customer or audience.

Why Do Business Processes Matter to IT?

IT does not exist in isolation. Every IT decision — which software to buy, what servers to set up, what systems to secure — must align with what the organization is actually trying to accomplish. An IT professional who doesn't understand business processes will build solutions that nobody needs, or miss the real problems entirely.

Core idea: IT is a means to an end. The "end" is always the business process. IT professionals serve business goals — not the other way around.

Types of Business Processes

Core Processes

Directly create value for the customer. E.g., manufacturing a product, delivering a service, processing an order.

Support Processes

Enable core processes to run. E.g., HR recruitment, IT infrastructure, accounting, facilities management.

Management Processes

Govern and oversee the organization. E.g., strategic planning, performance reviews, compliance monitoring.

How IT Supports Business Processes

  • Automation — IT eliminates manual, repetitive tasks (e.g., payroll systems, inventory tracking).
  • Communication — Email, video conferencing, and collaboration tools keep teams coordinated.
  • Data Management — Databases store and retrieve critical business information efficiently.
  • Decision Support — Analytics dashboards and reports help managers make informed choices.
  • Security & Compliance — IT protects process integrity and ensures regulatory requirements are met.

Real-World Example

Business Process Without IT With IT Support
Student enrollment Manual paper forms, long queues, errors in records Online enrollment portal, automatic slot management, instant confirmation
Bank transaction Teller-only, limited hours, paper receipts 24/7 ATM, mobile banking, instant notifications
Product inventory Manual counting, stock errors, delays in reorder Barcode/RFID scanning, auto-reorder triggers, real-time stock view

Topic 2

IT Environment

The IT environment refers to the complete set of hardware, software, networks, data, and people that an organization uses to manage information and support its operations. Think of it as the "ecosystem" in which all digital activity in an organization takes place.

IT Environment: The infrastructure and systems — physical and logical — that an organization uses to collect, store, process, and communicate information.

The 5 Basic Parts of a Typical IT Environment

1. Hardware

Physical devices — servers, computers, routers, switches, printers, storage drives, IoT devices. The "bones" of the IT environment.

2. Software

Programs that run on hardware — operating systems, business applications (ERP, CRM), custom-built apps, utilities.

3. Network

Infrastructure for communication — LAN, WAN, Wi-Fi, VPN, the internet. Connects all devices and enables data flow.

4. Data

The actual information assets — customer records, financial data, documents, logs. Often the most valuable part of the environment.

5. People

IT professionals, end-users, and administrators. Technology is only as effective as the people managing and using it.

How the Parts Work Together

These components are interdependent. A breakdown in one area affects the whole environment. For example:

  • If the network goes down, users can't access software hosted on servers (hardware), so data becomes inaccessible — and people can't work.
  • If people are not trained, even the best software and hardware will be misused or underutilized.

Types of IT Infrastructure

Type Description Example
On-Premise All hardware and software are physically located and managed within the organization. A school's dedicated server room
Cloud-Based Resources are hosted by a third-party provider and accessed via the internet. Google Workspace, Microsoft Azure
Hybrid Combination of on-premise and cloud infrastructure. Local student database + cloud backup

IT Environment and Security

Every component of the IT environment is a potential attack surface. IT professionals must assess and secure each layer:

  • Physical security — Who has access to server rooms and devices?
  • Network security — Firewalls, VPNs, intrusion detection systems.
  • Application security — Patching, authentication, input validation.
  • Data security — Encryption, access control, backup strategies.
  • User security — Training, awareness, and policy enforcement.

Topic 3

Organizational Culture

You can deploy the best technology in the world — and it will still fail if the organization's culture doesn't support it. Organizational culture is one of the most overlooked but most important factors in determining whether IT succeeds or struggles in any setting.

Organizational Culture: The shared values, beliefs, behaviors, norms, and assumptions that shape how people in an organization think, act, and interact — both with each other and with technology.

Elements of Organizational Culture

Values

What the organization believes is important — innovation, efficiency, tradition, collaboration.

Norms

Unwritten rules about how things are done. E.g., "We never skip approvals, even in emergencies."

Leadership Style

Top-down authority vs. collaborative decision-making directly affects how IT projects are approved and funded.

Communication Patterns

Whether teams share information freely or in silos determines how well IT systems get adopted.

How Culture Affects IT

Culture and IT constantly influence each other in both directions:

Cultural Characteristic Positive IT Impact Negative IT Impact
Innovation-friendly culture Embraces new tools, fast adoption of updates May lead to reckless experimentation, insecure shortcuts
Risk-averse / traditional culture Strong compliance, thorough testing before deployment Resistance to change, slow adoption, outdated systems
Collaborative culture Users openly report IT issues, IT gets real-world feedback Can slow decision-making if consensus is always needed
Siloed / hierarchical culture Clear lines of approval and authority for IT procurement Poor cross-department data sharing, redundant systems

Culture and IT Change Management

Introducing new IT systems is never just a technical challenge — it is a human challenge. IT professionals must anticipate cultural resistance when rolling out new systems. Common resistance patterns include:

  • "We've always done it this way" — Long-tenured employees comfortable with existing (often manual) workflows.
  • Fear of job displacement — Employees worrying that automation will replace their roles.
  • Low digital literacy — Staff who lack confidence in using new technology.
  • Lack of leadership buy-in — When management doesn't visibly support an IT initiative, staff won't either.
Key Insight for IT Professionals: Part of your job is understanding the humans, not just the technology. Successful IT implementation requires empathy, communication, and stakeholder management — not just technical skill.

Strategies for Aligning IT with Culture

  • Involve end-users early in the design and selection of new IT systems.
  • Provide thorough training and ongoing support during transitions.
  • Communicate clearly why a new system is being introduced and how it benefits users.
  • Secure visible leadership endorsement for new IT initiatives.
  • Start with pilot groups to build advocates before wide rollout.

Topic 4

Professionalism in IT

Being technically skilled is only half of being an IT professional. The other half — and often the more decisive half in the workplace — is professionalism. How you act, communicate, and carry yourself determines whether others trust you with critical systems and sensitive information.

Professionalism: The conduct, behavior, and attitude of a person in accordance with the norms, ethics, and expectations of their profession.

Core Traits of a Professional IT Person

Reliability

You do what you say you will do, when you said you would do it. Deadlines and commitments are honored.

Accountability

When something goes wrong, you own it. You don't pass blame or make excuses. You focus on fixing it.

Integrity

You act ethically even when no one is watching — no unauthorized access, no data misuse, no dishonest reporting.

Competence

You keep your skills updated. Technology changes fast — professionals commit to continuous learning.

Respect

You treat colleagues, clients, and users with dignity regardless of technical skill level or position.

Confidentiality

IT professionals often handle sensitive data. Professional behavior means never disclosing what you're not supposed to.

Professional Behavior in Practice

Situation Unprofessional Response Professional Response
A user reports a bug you caused "That's not my fault. I just followed the specs." "I'm sorry — let me look into it and fix it right away."
You gain access to a user's personal files during maintenance Browse files out of curiosity Access only what's necessary, document your actions, report anything unusual through proper channels
A client doesn't understand a technical explanation Get impatient, use more jargon, dismiss their concern Rephrase in plain language, use analogies, ask what part is unclear
Deadline is at risk due to unexpected issues Stay silent and hope it resolves itself Immediately notify stakeholders, present a revised timeline and plan

IT Codes of Ethics

Several professional bodies have established formal codes of ethics for IT professionals. These provide guidance on how to behave when faced with difficult situations:

  • ACM Code of Ethics — Emphasizes contributing to society, avoiding harm, honesty, fairness, and respecting privacy.
  • IEEE Code of Ethics — Focuses on public safety, honesty, avoiding conflicts of interest, and professional development.
  • ISOC (Internet Society) — Guides behavior around internet governance and access rights.
Why Ethics Matters Specifically in IT: IT professionals have access to systems and data that affect millions of people. A single unethical decision — misusing data, ignoring a security vulnerability, writing biased algorithms — can cause widespread harm. Your technical power comes with an equal measure of ethical responsibility.

Professionalism and Organizational Context

Professionalism in IT is not just about individual behavior — it shapes how the IT function is perceived and trusted within the organization:

  • IT teams that are transparent and responsive earn budget and leadership support more easily.
  • Professionals who communicate well get included in strategic decisions — not just technical tasks.
  • Consistent professional behavior builds the organizational trust that allows IT to drive real change.
  • Unprofessional behavior (poor communication, unreliability, privacy violations) leads to IT being sidelined or distrusted.

Continuous Professional Development

A key part of professionalism is never stopping the learning process. IT changes faster than almost any other field. Professionals stay current through:

  • Certifications — CompTIA, Cisco (CCNA), AWS, PMP, CISSP, etc.
  • Online learning platforms — Coursera, LinkedIn Learning, Udemy, freeCodeCamp
  • Industry events and communities — hackathons, tech conferences, open-source contributions
  • Reading — documentation, research papers, tech blogs, industry reports

Summary

Lesson Wrap-Up

This lesson established the organizational context within which IT professionals operate. Here's what to carry forward:

Topic Key Takeaway
Business Processes IT exists to support and improve business processes — not as a goal in itself. Always understand the business need first.
IT Environment A typical IT environment is made up of Hardware, Software, Network, Data, and People — all interdependent, all requiring care.
Organizational Culture Culture determines whether IT succeeds or fails. People adoption is as critical as technical deployment.
Professionalism Technical skill gets you hired. Professionalism — integrity, accountability, continuous growth — determines how far you go.
Final Thought: As BSIT students preparing to enter the industry, remember — you are not just learning systems and code. You are learning how to operate as trustworthy, ethical, and competent professionals within real organizations, serving real people, with real consequences. That context is everything.
