Context · Before Impressionism
What Art Looked Like Before

For centuries, European painting was ruled by the Académie des Beaux-Arts and its annual Paris Salon — the only gateway to a painter's career.

Their Rules
Grand Subjects Only — history, mythology, religion. Everyday life was beneath serious art.
Invisible Brushwork — paint blended to a porcelain finish. Never see a single stroke.
Studio Painting — composed from sketches indoors, far from nature and changing light.
Idealized Reality — figures beautified, stripped of all imperfection.
Strict Genre Hierarchy — history painting at the top, still lifes at the bottom.

Impressionism

"Let's get impressionable!" · c. 1860s–1880s

MonetRenoirDegasMorisot
What Was Their Main Goal?
The Fleeting Moment

In the 1860s, a bold group broke away from the rigid traditions of the Académie des Beaux-Arts. Instead of polished historical scenes, they wanted to capture something radical: the fleeting impression of a single moment.

The name "Impressionism" was born as an insult. After Monet exhibited Impression, Sunrise in 1874, critic Louis Leroy mockingly called them "Impressionists." The artists embraced it.

Impression, Sunrise
Claude Monet · 1872 · The painting that named the movement
"My wish is to depict what lies between the object and me."
— Claude Monet
Defining Approach
Painting En Plein Air

Rather than working from sketches in a dark studio, Impressionists carried their canvases outdoors — to parks, riversides, cafés, and gardens.

Light changes every minute. The same view at dawn looked different by noon. This urgency forced them to paint quickly — giving birth to loose, rapid brushwork.

Luncheon of the Boating Party
Pierre-Auguste Renoir · 1881 · The Phillips Collection
Direct observationNatural lightSpeed & spontaneityNo studio polish
Impressionism · Characteristics
Defining Characteristics
Part 1 of 2
🖌️
Visible Brushstrokes
Quick, loose, energetic marks that suggest rather than define. The brushstroke itself becomes expressive — you can see the painter's hand at work.
☀️
Light & Atmosphere
Capturing how light changes at different hours of the day. Monet painted the same haystacks dozens of times — each under different light conditions.
🌿
En Plein Air
Painting directly from nature, outdoors. The need to capture quickly before the light changed was the mother of their loose, impressionistic style.
Part 2 of 2
🎭
Everyday Modern Life
Parks, cafés, dancers, train stations. Ordinary people doing ordinary things — a radical departure from mythological and historical subjects.
🔵
Broken Color Technique
Small dabs of pure, unmixed color placed side by side. The eye blends them optically from a distance, creating a vibrancy that pre-mixed paint can't achieve.
🚫
No Black Shadows
Shadows are painted in color — blues, purples, greens — not black or grey. Shadows are full of reflected color from the environment.

Key Works
Gallery of Impressionism
The Cradle
Berthe Morisot · 1872 · Soft, intimate light

Morisot gave Impressionism something different: complete, quiet intimacy. A mother watching her sleeping baby — the kind of scene never before considered worthy of fine art.

The Ballet Class
Edgar Degas · c.1874 · Motion captured mid-moment

A ballet rehearsal — not a performance. Degas placed us at the back corner of the room, like we snuck in. Completely candid. He made you feel like a witness, not an audience.

Water Lilies
Claude Monet · 1906 · 250+ paintings in this series

250 paintings of the same pond. By the time Monet painted these final series, he was going blind. His failing vision accidentally pushed him toward something revolutionary — abstraction.

Movement 02
Post-Impressionism

"This is where things get interesting!" · c. 1886–1910s

Not one movement — but a radical explosion of individual visions.

Beyond the Fleeting Moment
What Changed?

☁️ Impressionism asked...

"What do I see?"
Captured the visual surface — light, color, atmosphere
The world as the eye perceives it at one instant

🧩 Post-Impressionism asked...

"What does it mean? How does it feel?"
Explored structure, emotion, symbolic meaning, permanence
Each artist developed a radically different personal style

British critic Roger Fry coined "Post-Impressionism" in 1910 as an umbrella term for these wildly different artists who all had one thing in common: they'd gone beyond Impressionism.

Artist Spotlight
Paul Cézanne
1839–1906 · "The Father of Modern Art"

Cézanne was obsessed with what lay beneath the surface. Where Impressionists saw shimmering light, Cézanne saw underlying geometry — cylinders, cones, spheres. He depicted scenes from multiple viewpoints simultaneously.

Still Life with Apples and Pears
Cézanne · c.1891 · Met Museum
Self-Portrait
Paul Cézanne · c.1875

Picasso said: "He is the father of us all." Cézanne's work directly led to Cubism.

Geometric simplificationMultiple viewpointsSolid, permanent form
Artist Spotlight
Vincent van Gogh
1853–1890 · "The Prophet of Emotion"
The Starry Night
Vincent van Gogh · 1889 · MoMA, New York

Van Gogh used swirling, turbulent brushstrokes and blazing colors to express raw psychological states. The Starry Night was painted from memory while in a mental asylum — the churning sky is not a description. It is the feeling.

He sold only one painting in his lifetime. Today his works are worth hundreds of millions.

"I dream of painting and then I paint my dream."
— Vincent van Gogh
Artist Spotlight
Paul Gauguin
1848–1903 · "The Symbolist & Primitivist"

Disillusioned with Western civilization, Gauguin fled to Tahiti in search of a more authentic existence. His paintings use flat, bold color areas, thick dark outlines, and symbolic figures from Polynesian life.

Where Do We Come From? What Are We? Where Are We Going?
Paul Gauguin · 1897–98 · Museum of Fine Arts, Boston
Flat color planesSymbolic contentNon-Western influenceSpiritual themes
Artist Spotlight (Bonus)
Georges Seurat
1859–1891 · Inventor of Pointillism
A Sunday Afternoon on the Island of La Grande Jatte
Georges Seurat · 1884–1886 · Art Institute of Chicago

Pointillism — thousands of tiny dots of pure color — blends in the viewer's eye, not on the canvas. This painting took two years to complete. Every centimeter — dots. The figures feel frozen, monumental, eternal.

Movement 03
LES FAUVES
The Wild Beasts

In 1905, critic Louis Vauxcelles saw Matisse's paintings and declared: "Donatello among the wild beasts!" The name stuck.

Core Idea
Color as Pure Emotion
Woman with a Hat
Henri Matisse · 1905 · The painting that shocked Paris · SFMOMA

In a Fauvist painting, color is completely freed from its descriptive duty. A face can be green. A sky can be red. Why? Because that's the emotional truth of the moment.

This was the first major modern movement to fully liberate color from nature. Color no longer serves form — color is the art.

These colors would never appear together in nature — that was the point.

Key Features · Part 1 of 2
🎨
Non-Naturalistic Color
Colors bear no relationship to how objects actually look. A green face, a red river — chosen for emotional resonance, not description.
💥
Clashing, Vibrating Color
Complementary colors side by side — red next to green, orange next to blue — creating maximum visual tension and energy. The painting almost hums.
📐
Simplified, Flat Forms
Influenced by Gauguin's flatness. Reduced shapes, minimal shading, little interest in realistic depth. Flatness lets color dominate entirely.
Key Features · Part 2 of 2
😤
Pure Emotional Expression
Color is chosen to convey mood, feeling, and energy — not to describe visual reality. The viewer feels the painting before they understand it.
⚡
Bold, Energetic Brushwork
Rough, spontaneous marks. No smooth blending — raw, expressive strokes that add to the painting's physical energy.
🌐
Liberation from Nature
Art does not need to represent reality. This was Fauvism's revolutionary gift — absolute artistic freedom was not just acceptable, but necessary.
Artist Spotlight
Henri Matisse
1869–1954 · Leader of the Fauves

Matisse believed color should work like music — pure, emotional, direct. His work feels joyful, liberated, alive.

Dance (I)
Henri Matisse · 1909 · MoMA, New York

Dance — five simplified red bodies on pure blue and green. No detail. No shading. Just rhythm, color, and movement.

"I used color as a means of expressing my emotion and not as a transcription of nature."
— Henri Matisse
More Fauvist Artists
Derain & Vlaminck
Charing Cross Bridge, London
André Derain · 1906 · A river of impossible oranges & pinks

Derain painted London — famously grey — in violent oranges and electric pinks. None of this is what London looks like. But you feel the city's energy more here than in any photograph.

Restaurant de la Machine à Bougival
Maurice de Vlaminck · c.1905 · Musée d'Orsay

Vlaminck declared he wanted to "burn down the École des Beaux-Arts with my cobalts and vermilions." These weren't timid experiments — they were war cries.

Legacy · Part 1 of 2
📐
Cézanne → Cubism
Cézanne's geometric simplification directly inspired Picasso and Braque. Cubism couldn't have existed without him. Picasso called him "the father of us all."
💔
Van Gogh → Expressionism
Van Gogh's distortion of reality to express inner states became the foundation of German Expressionism. Munch's The Scream is pure Van Gogh philosophy.
🌍
Gauguin → Primitivism
Gauguin opened Western art to African, Asian, and Oceanic influences — a seismic shift that changed everything. Picasso was directly inspired by African masks.
Legacy · Part 2 of 2
🌈
Matisse → Color Field Art
Matisse's liberated color led directly to Abstract Expressionism and Color Field painters like Mark Rothko. People stand in front of Rothko's canvases and cry.
🎭
All Three → Modern Design
Every app icon, logo, movie poster, and website layout that uses bold color to create mood carries a piece of these movements. Flat forms. Expressive color. Simplified shapes.
🔓
The Big Idea
Art does NOT have to look like reality. The artist's vision, feeling, and intention matter more than accuracy. This is the founding idea of all modern art.
