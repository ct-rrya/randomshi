Context · Before Impressionism
What Art Looked Like Before

For centuries, European painting was ruled by the Académie des Beaux-Arts and its annual Paris Salon — the only gateway to a painter's career.

Their Rules
Grand Subjects Only — history, mythology, religion. Everyday life was beneath art.
Invisible Brushwork — paint blended to a porcelain finish. Never see a stroke.
Studio Painting — composed from sketches indoors, away from nature.
Idealized Reality — figures beautified, stripped of imperfection.
Strict Genre Hierarchy — history painting at top, still lifes at bottom.
"The closer to reality, the better. The more invisible the hand, the more skilled the artist."
— The Academic tradition, 200+ years
Academic Art — The Gold Standard
This Was "Good Art"

These are the paintings the Salon celebrated — what the Impressionists dared to defy.

Impressionism
What Was Their Main Goal?

In the 1860s, a bold group of French painters broke away from the rigid traditions of the Académie des Beaux-Arts. They wanted to capture the fleeting impression of a single moment.

The name "Impressionism" was born as an insult. After Monet exhibited Impression, Sunrise in 1874, critic Louis Leroy mocked them. The artists embraced it. History proved him wrong.

Impression, Sunrise
Claude Monet · 1872 · The painting that named the movement
"My wish is to depict what lies between the object and me."
— Claude Monet
Defining Approach
Painting En Plein Air

Rather than working in a dark studio, Impressionists carried their canvases outdoors — to parks, riversides, cafés, and gardens.

Light changes every minute. This urgency forced them to paint fast — giving birth to loose, rapid brushwork.

Luncheon of the Boating Party
Pierre-Auguste Renoir · 1881 · The Phillips Collection
Direct observationNatural lightSpeed & spontaneityNo studio polish
Characteristics · Part 1 of 2
Defining Characteristics
🖌️
Visible Brushstrokes
Quick, loose, energetic marks. The brushstroke itself becomes expressive — you can see the painter's hand at work.
☀️
Light & Atmosphere
Capturing how light changes at different hours. Monet painted the same haystacks dozens of times — each under different light conditions.
🌿
En Plein Air
Painting directly from nature, outdoors. The need to capture quickly before the light changed was the mother of their impressionistic style.
Characteristics · Part 2 of 2
Defining Characteristics
🎭
Everyday Modern Life
Parks, cafés, dancers, train stations. Ordinary people doing ordinary things — a radical departure from mythological subjects.
🔵
Broken Color Technique
Tiny dabs of pure, unmixed color placed side by side. The eye blends them optically, creating vibrancy that pre-mixed paint can't achieve.
🚫
No Black Shadows
Shadows painted in blues, purples, greens — not black or grey. Shadows are full of reflected color from the environment.
Deep-Dive
Brushstrokes: Before & After
⚖️ Academic — Invisible Brushwork
Le Retour de la Moisson
Bouguereau · 1878 · Mirror-smooth, no trace of the brush

The Académie des Beaux-Arts demanded paint be blended to a porcelain finish. You should never see a brushstroke — the artist's hand must be invisible. Form, outline, and classical precision above all.

✨ Impressionism — Brushstroke as Expression
Water Lilies
Monet · 1906 · Every stroke is visible, suggestive, alive

Monet's loose, thick dabs pulse with energy. The stroke is the water, the light, the motion. Van Gogh pushed this even further — his swirling marks in Starry Night become pure psychological turbulence.

Post-Impressionism
What Changed?

☁️ Impressionism asked...

"What do I see?" — the visual surface
Light, color, atmosphere at one instant

🧩 Post-Impressionism asked...

"What does it mean? How does it feel?"
Structure, emotion, symbolic meaning, permanence
Each artist developed a radically different personal style

Roger Fry coined "Post-Impressionism" in 1910 as a term for wildly different artists who all had one thing in common: they'd gone beyond Impressionism.

Artist Spotlight
Paul Cézanne
1839–1906 · "The Father of Modern Art"

Cézanne saw underlying geometry — cylinders, cones, spheres. He depicted scenes from multiple viewpoints simultaneously.

Still Life
Cézanne · c.1891
Self-Portrait
Cézanne · c.1875

Picasso said: "He is the father of us all." Cézanne's work directly led to Cubism.

Geometric simplificationMultiple viewpointsSolid, permanent form
Artist Spotlight
Vincent van Gogh
1853–1890 · "The Prophet of Emotion"
The Starry Night
Vincent van Gogh · 1889 · MoMA, New York

Van Gogh used swirling, turbulent brushstrokes and blazing colors to express raw psychological states. The Starry Night was painted from memory while in a mental asylum — the churning sky is the feeling.

He sold only one painting in his lifetime. Today his works are worth hundreds of millions.

"I dream of painting and then I paint my dream."
— Vincent van Gogh
Artist Spotlight
Paul Gauguin
1848–1903 · "The Symbolist & Primitivist"

Disillusioned with Western civilization, Gauguin fled to Tahiti. His paintings use flat, bold color areas, thick dark outlines, and symbolic figures from Polynesian life.

Where Do We Come From? What Are We? Where Are We Going?
Paul Gauguin · 1897–98 · Museum of Fine Arts, Boston
Flat color planesSymbolic contentNon-Western influenceSpiritual themes
Artist Spotlight (Bonus)
Georges Seurat
1859–1891 · Inventor of Pointillism
A Sunday Afternoon on the Island of La Grande Jatte
Georges Seurat · 1884–1886 · Art Institute of Chicago

Pointillism — thousands of tiny dots of pure color that blend in the viewer's eye, not on the canvas. This painting took two years. Every centimeter — dots. The figures feel frozen, monumental, eternal.

Movement 03
LES FAUVES
The Wild Beasts

In 1905, critic Louis Vauxcelles declared: "Donatello among the wild beasts!" The name stuck.

c. 1905–1908 · Get ready for a burst of color!

Core Idea
Color as Pure Emotion
Woman with a Hat
Henri Matisse · 1905 · The painting that shocked Paris · SFMOMA

In a Fauvist painting, color is completely freed from its descriptive duty. A face can be green. A sky can be red. Because that's the emotional truth of the moment.

The first major modern movement to fully liberate color from nature. Color no longer serves form — color is the art.

These colors would never appear together in nature — that was the point.

Key Features · Part 1 of 2
Key Features
🎨
Non-Naturalistic Color
Colors bear no relationship to how objects look. A green face, a red river — chosen for emotional resonance, not description.
💥
Clashing, Vibrating Color
Complementary colors side by side — red next to green, orange next to blue — creating maximum visual tension. The painting almost hums.
📐
Simplified, Flat Forms
Influenced by Gauguin's flatness. Reduced shapes, minimal shading. Flatness lets color dominate entirely.
Key Features · Part 2 of 2
Key Features
😤
Pure Emotional Expression
Color chosen to convey mood, feeling, and energy. The viewer feels the painting before they understand it.
⚡
Bold, Energetic Brushwork
Rough, spontaneous marks. No smooth blending — raw, expressive strokes that add physical energy to the painting.
🌐
Liberation from Nature
Art does not need to represent reality. This was Fauvism's revolutionary gift — absolute artistic freedom was not just acceptable, but necessary.
Artist Spotlight
Henri Matisse
1869–1954 · Leader of the Fauves

Matisse believed color should work like music — pure, emotional, direct. His work feels joyful, liberated, alive.

Dance (I)
Matisse · 1909 · MoMA
Woman with a Hat
Matisse · 1905 · SFMOMA

Dance — five simplified red bodies on pure blue and green. No detail. No shading. Just rhythm, color, and movement.

"I used color as a means of expressing my emotion and not as a transcription of nature."
— Henri Matisse
More Fauvist Artists
Derain & Vlaminck
Charing Cross Bridge, London
André Derain · 1906 · A river of impossible oranges & pinks

Derain painted London — famously grey — in violent oranges and electric pinks. You feel the city's energy more here than in any photograph.

Restaurant de la Machine à Bougival
Maurice de Vlaminck · c.1905 · Musée d'Orsay

Vlaminck wanted to "burn down the École des Beaux-Arts with my cobalts and vermilions." These weren't experiments — they were war cries.

Impressionism → Post

Kept visible brushstrokes and color freedom, but added depth — structure, psychological meaning. Less about what the eye catches, more about what the mind holds.

Post-Imp → Fauvism

Van Gogh's emotional color and Gauguin's flat boldness inspired the Fauves to take the final step: complete liberation. Color no longer describes — it expresses.

Legacy
Impact on All of Modern Art
📐
Cézanne → Cubism
Cézanne's geometric simplification directly inspired Picasso and Braque. Cubism couldn't have existed without him. Picasso called him "the father of us all."
💔
Van Gogh → Expressionism
Van Gogh's distortion of reality to express inner states became the foundation of German Expressionism. Munch's The Scream is pure Van Gogh philosophy.
🌍
Gauguin → Primitivism
Gauguin opened Western art to African, Asian, and Oceanic influences — a seismic shift. Picasso was directly inspired by African masks.
🌈
Matisse → Color Field Art
Matisse's liberated color led to Abstract Expressionism and Color Field painters like Mark Rothko. People stand in front of Rothko's canvases and cry.
🎭
All Three → Modern Design
Every app icon, logo, poster, and website that uses bold color to create mood carries a piece of these movements. Flat forms. Expressive color. Simplified shapes.
🔓
The Big Idea
Art does NOT have to look like reality. The artist's vision, feeling, and intention matter more than accuracy. This is the founding idea of all modern art.
