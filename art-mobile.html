Impressionism

"Let's get impressionable!"
c. 1860s–1880s, France

Impressionism
What Was Their Main Goal?
Breaking the Rules
Impression, Sunrise — Monet, 1872
The painting that named the movement
🔍 Tap

In the 1860s, bold French painters broke from the Académie des Beaux-Arts to capture the fleeting impression of a single moment.

The name was an insult — critic Louis Leroy mocked Monet's 1874 exhibit. The artists embraced it.

"My wish is to depict what lies between the object and me."
— Claude Monet
Impressionism
Defining Approach
Painting En Plein Air
Luncheon of the Boating Party — Renoir, 1881
The Phillips Collection
🔍 Tap

Instead of dark studios, Impressionists carried canvases outdoors — to parks, riversides, and cafés.

Light changes every minute. This urgency forced them to paint fast — giving birth to their loose, rapid brushwork.

Direct observation Natural light Speed & spontaneity No studio polish
Impressionism · Characteristics
Defining Characteristics · 1 of 2
What Made It Different
🖌️
Visible Brushstrokes
Quick, loose, energetic marks that suggest rather than define. The brushstroke itself becomes expressive — you can see the hand at work.
☀️
Light & Atmosphere
Capturing light as it changes by the hour. Monet painted the same haystacks dozens of times — each under different conditions.
🌿
En Plein Air
Painting directly from nature outdoors. The urgency of changing light was the mother of their impressionistic style.
Impressionism · Characteristics
Defining Characteristics · 2 of 2
🎭
Everyday Modern Life
Parks, cafés, dancers, train stations. Ordinary people doing ordinary things — a radical break from mythological subjects.
🔵
Broken Color Technique
Small dabs of pure, unmixed color placed side by side. The eye blends them at a distance, creating vibrancy paint mixing can't achieve.
🚫
No Black Shadows
Shadows are painted in blues, purples, greens — not black. They observed that shadows are full of reflected environmental color.

Beyond the Fleeting Moment

The world at one instant

British critic Roger Fry coined "Post-Impressionism" in 1910 — an umbrella for artists who'd gone beyond.

Artist Spotlight
Artist Spotlight
Paul Cézanne
1839–1906 · The Father of Modern Art
Still Life with Apples and Pears
Paul Cézanne · c.1891 · Metropolitan Museum of Art
🔍 Tap

Where Impressionists saw shimmering light, Cézanne saw underlying geometry — cylinders, cones, spheres.

He depicted the same scene from multiple viewpoints at once. Picasso said: "He is the father of us all." His work led directly to Cubism.

Geometric simplification Multiple viewpoints Permanent form
Artist Spotlight
Artist Spotlight
Vincent van Gogh
1853–1890 · The Prophet of Emotion
The Starry Night · 1889 · MoMA, New York
Painted from memory while in a mental asylum
🔍 Tap

Van Gogh used swirling, turbulent brushstrokes and blazing, non-natural colors to express raw psychological states.

He sold only one painting in his lifetime. Today his works are worth hundreds of millions. He directly inspired Expressionism.

"I dream of painting and then I paint my dream."
— Vincent van Gogh
Artist Spotlight
Artist Spotlight
Paul Gauguin
1848–1903 · The Symbolist & Primitivist
Where Do We Come From? What Are We?
Paul Gauguin · 1897–98 · Museum of Fine Arts, Boston
🔍 Tap

Disillusioned with Western civilization, Gauguin fled to Tahiti. He believed "primitive" cultures held a spiritual truth Europe had lost.

His paintings use flat, bold color areas and thick outlines from Polynesian life — opening Western art to the world beyond Europe.

Flat color planesSymbolic content Non-Western influenceSpiritual themes
Artist Spotlight
Artist Spotlight · Bonus
Georges Seurat
1859–1891 · Inventor of Pointillism
A Sunday Afternoon on La Grande Jatte
Georges Seurat · 1884–1886 · Art Institute of Chicago
🔍 Tap

Pointillism — thousands of tiny dots of pure color — was based on optical theory: the dots blend in your eye, not on canvas.

The result: Impressionist subject matter with an almost timeless, monumental quality. Frozen. Classical. Eternal.

In 1905, critic Louis Vauxcelles saw Matisse's paintings and declared: "Donatello among the wild beasts!"

Fauvism
Color as Pure Emotion
Freed from Nature
Woman with a Hat — Matisse, 1905
The painting that shocked Paris · SFMOMA
🔍 Tap

The Fauves took Van Gogh's emotional color and Gauguin's boldness further than anyone had dared.

Color is freed from its descriptive duty. A face can be green. A sky red. A river violet — because that's the emotional truth.

These colors would never appear together in nature — that was the point.

Fauvism · Key Features
Key Features · 1 of 2
What Made It Wild
🎨
Non-Naturalistic Color
Colors bear no relationship to how things look. A green face, a red river — chosen for emotional resonance, not description.
💥
Clashing, Vibrating Color
Complementary colors placed side by side — red next to green, orange next to blue — creating maximum visual tension and energy.
📐
Simplified, Flat Forms
Reduced shapes, minimal shading, little interest in realistic three-dimensional depth — influenced by Gauguin's flatness.
Fauvism · Key Features
Key Features · 2 of 2
😤
Pure Emotional Expression
Color conveys mood and feeling — not visual reality. The viewer feels the painting before they understand it.
⚡
Bold, Energetic Brushwork
Rough, spontaneous marks. No smooth blending — raw, expressive strokes that add to the painting's physical energy.
🌐
Liberation from Nature
Fauvism's revolutionary gift: absolute artistic freedom was not just acceptable, but necessary. Art does not need to look like reality.
Artist Spotlight
Artist Spotlight
Henri Matisse
1869–1954 · Leader of the Fauves
Dance (I) — Henri Matisse, 1909
MoMA, New York
🔍 Tap

Matisse believed color should work like music — pure, emotional, direct. Dance: five red figures on blue and green. No detail. Just rhythm, color, movement.

"I used color as a means of expressing my emotion and not as a transcription of nature."
— Henri Matisse

Impressionism → Post-Impressionism
Kept visible brushstrokes and color freedom, but added depth — structure, psychological meaning, personal philosophy.
Post-Impressionism → Fauvism
Van Gogh's emotional color and Gauguin's boldness inspired the Fauves to take the final step: complete liberation. Reality is optional.
Legacy
Legacy
Impact on All of Modern Art
📐
Cézanne → Cubism
Cézanne's geometric simplification directly inspired Picasso and Braque. Cubism couldn't exist without him.
💔
Van Gogh → Expressionism
His distortion of reality for emotional expression became the foundation of German Expressionism (Munch, Kirchner).
🌍
Gauguin → Primitivism
Opened Western art to African, Asian, and Oceanic influences — a seismic shift that changed everything.
🌈
Matisse → Color Field Art
His liberated color led to Abstract Expressionism and Color Field painters like Mark Rothko.
🔓
The Big Idea
Art does NOT have to look like reality. The artist's vision matters more than accuracy. This is the founding idea of all modern art.
