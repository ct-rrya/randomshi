I
Movement 01
Impressionism

"Let's get impressionable!"
c. 1860s–1880s, France

MonetRenoirDegas MorisotPissarroSisley
Impressionism
What Was Their Main Goal?

In the 1860s, a bold group of French painters broke away from the rigid traditions of the Académie des Beaux-Arts. Instead of perfectly polished historical scenes, they wanted to capture something radical: the fleeting impression of a single moment.

The name "Impressionism" was actually born as an insult. After Monet exhibited Impression, Sunrise in 1874, critic Louis Leroy mockingly called them "Impressionists." The artists embraced it — and history proved him wrong.

"My wish is to depict what lies between the object and me."
— Claude Monet
Impression, Sunrise
Claude Monet · 1872 · The painting that named the movement
Luncheon of the Boating Party
Pierre-Auguste Renoir · 1881 · The Phillips Collection
Defining Approach
Painting En Plein Air

Rather than working from sketches in a dark studio, Impressionists carried their canvases outdoors — to parks, riversides, cafés, and gardens.

Painting in the open air meant confronting something no studio painter had to face: light changes every minute. The same view at dawn looked completely different by noon. This urgency forced them to paint quickly — giving birth to their distinctive loose, rapid brushwork.

Direct observation Natural light Speed & spontaneity No studio polish

Characteristic Deep-Dive
Visible, Rapid Brushstrokes

Before Impressionism, "good" painting meant smooth, invisible brushwork — you weren't supposed to see the artist's hand. The Impressionists broke this rule completely.

Their marks are bold, visible, and energetic. Short dabs, thick impasto strokes, directional marks that follow the form of waves, leaves, or fabric. The brushstroke itself communicates energy and motion.

Look at Van Gogh's later work — he took this to its logical extreme, making the brushstroke the entire emotional language of the painting.

II
Movement 02
Post-Impressionism

"This is where things get interesting!"
c. 1886–1910s

Not one movement — but a radical explosion of individual visions, each pushing beyond what Impressionism could express.

Post-Impressionism
Beyond the Fleeting Moment

☁️ Impressionism asked...

"What do I see?"
Captured the visual surface — light, color, atmosphere
The world as the eye perceives it at one instant

🧩 Post-Impressionism asked...

"What does it mean? How does it feel?"
Explored structure, emotion, symbolic meaning, permanence
Each artist developed a radically different personal style

British critic Roger Fry coined "Post-Impressionism" in 1910 as an umbrella term for these diverse, wildly different artists who all had one thing in common: they'd gone beyond Impressionism.

Artist Spotlight
Paul Cézanne
1839–1906 · "The Father of Modern Art"

Cézanne was obsessed with what lay beneath the surface. Where Impressionists saw shimmering light, Cézanne saw underlying geometry — cylinders, cones, spheres.

He often depicted the same scene from multiple viewpoints simultaneously, creating a flattened, analytical space. His still lifes feel monumental; his landscapes like architectural blueprints.

Picasso said: "He is the father of us all." Cézanne's work directly led to Cubism.

Geometric simplificationMultiple viewpointsSolid, permanent form
Still Life with Apples and Pears
Paul Cézanne · c.1891 · Metropolitan Museum of Art
Self-Portrait
Paul Cézanne · c.1875
The Starry Night
Vincent van Gogh · 1889 · MoMA, New York
Self-Portrait
Vincent van Gogh · 1889
Artist Spotlight
Vincent van Gogh
1853–1890 · "The Prophet of Emotion"

Van Gogh used swirling, turbulent brushstrokes and blazing, non-natural colors to express raw psychological states — joy, anguish, longing, ecstasy.

Look at The Starry Night: painted from memory while in a mental asylum, the churning sky is not a description — it is the feeling. Van Gogh directly inspired Expressionism.

He sold only one painting in his lifetime. Today his works are worth hundreds of millions.

"I dream of painting and then I paint my dream."
— Vincent van Gogh
Artist Spotlight
Paul Gauguin
1848–1903 · "The Symbolist & Primitivist"

Disillusioned with Western civilization, Gauguin fled to Tahiti in search of a more authentic existence. He believed "primitive" cultures possessed a spiritual truth that industrial Europe had lost.

His paintings use flat, bold color areas, thick dark outlines, and symbolic figures from Polynesian life. The style influenced Symbolism, Fauvism, and opened Western art to the world beyond Europe.

Flat color planesSymbolic content Non-Western influenceSpiritual themes
Where Do We Come From? What Are We? Where Are We Going?
Paul Gauguin · 1897–98 · Museum of Fine Arts, Boston
A Sunday Afternoon on the Island of La Grande Jatte
Georges Seurat · 1884–1886 · Art Institute of Chicago
Artist Spotlight (Bonus)
Georges Seurat
1859–1891 · Inventor of Pointillism

Seurat took the Impressionist broken-color technique and made it scientific. Pointillism — applying thousands of tiny dots of pure color — was based on optical theories: the dots blend in the viewer's eye, not on the canvas.

The result? Impressionist subject matter given an almost timeless, monumental quality. The figures feel frozen, classical, eternal.

This is Impressionism taken to its logical, mathematical extreme — and beyond.

Movement 03
LES FAUVES
The Wild Beasts

c. 1905–1908 · Get ready for a burst of color!

In 1905, critic Louis Vauxcelles saw Matisse's paintings surrounding a classical sculpture and declared: "Donatello among the wild beasts!" The name stuck.

Fauvism
Color as Pure Emotion

The Fauves were inspired by Van Gogh's emotional color and Gauguin's bold flatness — but they took everything further than anyone had dared.

In a Fauvist painting, color is completely freed from its descriptive duty. A face can be green. A sky can be red. A river can be violet. Why? Because that's the emotional truth of the moment.

This was the first major modern movement to fully liberate color from nature. Color no longer serves form — color is the art.

These colors would never appear together in nature — that was the point.

Woman with a Hat
Henri Matisse · 1905 · The painting that shocked Paris · SFMOMA
Fauvism · Key Features
Key Features
Part 1 of 2
🎨
Non-Naturalistic Color
Colors bear no relationship to how objects actually look. A green face, a red river — chosen for emotional resonance, not description.
💥
Clashing, Vibrating Color
Complementary colors placed side by side — red next to green, orange next to blue — creating maximum visual tension and energy.
📐
Simplified, Flat Forms
Influenced by Gauguin's flatness. Reduced shapes, minimal shading, little interest in realistic three-dimensional depth.
Fauvism · Key Features
Key Features
Part 2 of 2
😤
Pure Emotional Expression
Color is chosen to convey mood, feeling, and energy — not to describe visual reality. The viewer feels the painting before they understand it.
⚡
Bold, Energetic Brushwork
Rough, spontaneous marks. No smooth blending — raw, expressive strokes that add to the painting's physical energy.
🌐
Liberation from Nature
Art does not need to represent reality. This was Fauvism's revolutionary gift — absolute artistic freedom was not just acceptable, but necessary.
Artist Spotlight
Henri Matisse
1869–1954 · Leader of the Fauves

Matisse was Fauvism's greatest voice. He believed color should produce the same effect as music — pure, emotional, direct. His work feels joyful, liberated, alive.

Dance (1909) shows five figures in a circle — nothing but simplified red bodies on pure blue and green. No detail. No shading. Just rhythm, color, and movement.

"I used color as a means of expressing my emotion and not as a transcription of nature."
— Henri Matisse

Derain painted London's bridges in violent oranges and electric pinks. Vlaminck declared he wanted to "burn down the École des Beaux-Arts with my cobalts and vermilions." These weren't timid experiments — they were war cries.
